The performance degradation observed in high-dimensional history stacking (Condition C) is primarily due to the "curse of dimensionality" in the policy's input space, which dilutes the signal-to-noise ratio of the state representation. I hypothesize that a **Temporal Attention-based Actor-Critic (TAAC)** architecture—which uses a lightweight self-attention mechanism over a sliding window of the last $k=10$ actions and states—will outperform both the flat MLP (Condition B) and the recurrent GRU (Condition C) by selectively weighting relevant past actions while maintaining a fixed, manageable input dimensionality. Unlike the GRU, which suffers from vanishing gradients and hidden state initialization issues in short-horizon RL, the attention mechanism provides a direct, permutation-invariant path for the policy to correlate specific past actions with current state transitions, leading to superior robustness under stochastic latency regimes without requiring the massive sample sizes needed for recurrent convergence.
