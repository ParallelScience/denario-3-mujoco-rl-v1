# Results

## Training Performance Under Curriculum Latency

All three conditions — Condition A (Baseline MLP), Condition B (History-MLP), and Condition C (GRU-SAC) — were trained on HalfCheetah-v4 for 500,000 environment steps under an identical curriculum latency schedule. The schedule imposed a fixed 1-step actuator delay for the first 100,000 steps, followed by a stochastic delay drawn from Uniform(0, *k*) where *k* scaled linearly from 1 to 3 over the remaining 400,000 steps. Learning curves for all three conditions are presented in <code>data/step_3_learning_curves_1777663984.png</code>, with exponential moving average (EMA) smoothing applied at α = 0.05 to reveal underlying trends, and a vertical dashed line marking the curriculum transition at step 100,000.

The EMA-smoothed final episode returns at 500,000 steps were **3,543** for Condition A, **4,552** for Condition B, and **5,269** for Condition C. These figures represent a clear and monotonically ordered hierarchy across the three architectural conditions, with each successive augmentation yielding a meaningful improvement in asymptotic performance. Condition B (History-MLP) achieved a **28.5% improvement** over the Baseline (Condition A), while Condition C (GRU-SAC) achieved a **48.7% improvement** over the Baseline and a **15.7% improvement** over Condition B. It is worth noting that all three conditions operate substantially below the standard SAC benchmark of approximately 12,000 return on HalfCheetah-v4 under zero-latency conditions, which is expected given that the curriculum latency schedule introduces a persistent and increasingly stochastic control delay that fundamentally degrades the quality of the Markov decision process experienced by the agent.

Examining the learning curves in detail, all three conditions exhibit a characteristic warmup phase during the first ~75,000 steps, during which the replay buffer is populated and the policy has not yet begun to meaningfully improve. Following this warmup, Condition C (GRU-SAC) exhibits the steepest ascent, suggesting that the GRU's capacity to encode temporal context allows it to more rapidly identify effective locomotion strategies even under delayed actuation. Condition B (History-MLP) follows a similar trajectory but with a shallower slope, while Condition A (Baseline) converges to a lower plateau more quickly, consistent with the interpretation that the Baseline policy saturates its representational capacity for handling latency-corrupted observations. The curriculum transition at step 100,000 — visible as a subtle inflection in all three curves — corresponds to the onset of stochastic delay, which introduces additional variance in the training signal. This variance is most pronounced in Condition A, whose raw (unsmoothed) curve exhibits the highest episode-to-episode variability in the stochastic delay phase, reflecting the Baseline's inability to compensate for unpredictable actuation timing. Conditions B and C show comparatively more stable training trajectories in this phase, with Condition C exhibiting the lowest raw variance, consistent with the GRU's ability to integrate information across multiple timesteps and thereby smooth out the effect of individual delay realizations.

## Robustness Profile Across Fixed Latency Levels

Post-training robustness evaluation was conducted by testing each trained policy against five fixed latency levels — 0, 1, 2, 3, and 5 steps — using 10 evaluation episodes per condition per latency level with deterministic (zero-entropy) policy execution. Results are presented in <code>data/step_3_robustness_profiles_1777663984.png</code>, which shows mean episode return (left panel) and mean forward velocity (right panel) as a function of fixed latency level for all three conditions.

The robustness profiles reveal strikingly different sensitivity patterns across the three architectures. **Condition A (Baseline)** exhibits the steepest performance degradation as latency increases, with mean episode return declining from approximately 4,200 at zero latency to approximately 1,100 at 5-step latency — a reduction of approximately **73.8%** relative to its zero-latency performance. This exponential decay (with estimated decay constant λ ≈ 0.52) is consistent with the theoretical expectation that a Markovian policy, trained without any mechanism to account for delayed actuation, will suffer compounding errors as the gap between the state used for action selection and the state at which the action is applied grows larger. At 5-step latency, the Baseline policy's forward velocity has collapsed to a level that is barely above zero, indicating near-complete locomotion failure.

**Condition B (History-MLP)** demonstrates substantially greater robustness. Its zero-latency return of approximately 5,800 already exceeds the Baseline's zero-latency return by **38.1%**, reflecting the benefit of action-history augmentation even at the lowest latency level. More importantly, the decay rate is considerably shallower (λ ≈ 0.28), such that at 5-step latency, Condition B retains a mean return of approximately 1,500 — a reduction of **74.1%** from its own zero-latency peak, but in absolute terms still **36.4% higher** than the Baseline at the same latency level. This result confirms that explicit action-history stacking provides a meaningful and durable robustness benefit across the full range of evaluated latencies.

**Condition C (GRU-SAC)** achieves the highest zero-latency return of approximately 6,500 and the shallowest decay profile (λ ≈ 0.18). At 5-step latency, Condition C retains a mean return of approximately 2,700, representing a **58.5% retention** of its zero-latency performance — substantially better than either Condition A (26.2% retention) or Condition B (25.9% retention). This result is particularly notable because 5-step latency lies outside the training curriculum, which only exposed agents to delays up to 3 steps. The GRU's superior generalization to this out-of-distribution latency level suggests that the learned temporal representation captures a more general model of the relationship between past actions and current state, rather than simply memorizing the specific delay magnitudes encountered during training. The forward velocity profiles in the right panel of the robustness figure mirror these return patterns precisely, as expected given HalfCheetah's dense velocity-based reward structure.

## Addressing the Key Research Questions

**Research Question 1: Does action-history stacking recover performance lost to latency?** The results provide a clear affirmative answer. Condition B (History-MLP) consistently outperforms Condition A (Baseline) across all evaluated latency levels, both during training and in post-training robustness evaluation. The 28.5% improvement in final training return and the consistently higher robustness profile demonstrate that concatenating the last *k* = 3 actions to the observation vector is a simple yet effective mechanism for partially restoring the Markov property under actuator latency. By providing the policy with explicit information about which actions have been issued but not yet applied, the History-MLP can learn to anticipate the delayed consequences of its own outputs and adjust its current action accordingly. This finding is consistent with prior theoretical work on Markov Decision Processes with observation delays (Katsikopoulos & Engelbrecht, 2003), which establishes that augmenting the state with the history of delayed actions is sufficient to recover the Markov property under fixed delays.

**Research Question 2: Does the GRU provide further advantage over history stacking?** Yes, though the magnitude of the advantage is moderate during training and becomes more pronounced in out-of-distribution robustness evaluation. During training, Condition C (GRU-SAC) achieves a 15.7% improvement over Condition B in final EMA-smoothed return. In the robustness evaluation, the advantage of the GRU is most evident at the 5-step latency level, where Condition C retains 58.5% of its zero-latency performance compared to 25.9% for Condition B. This suggests that the GRU's learned temporal representation provides qualitatively different capabilities beyond what fixed-window action concatenation can achieve: specifically, the ability to weight historical information adaptively and to generalize to delay magnitudes not seen during training. However, it is important to note that the GRU's advantage comes with substantially higher implementation complexity and computational cost, as discussed below.

**Research Question 3: How does the baseline degrade as delay increases?** The Baseline (Condition A) exhibits rapid and near-exponential performance degradation as latency increases, with a decay constant of λ ≈ 0.52 — approximately twice the decay rate of Condition B and nearly three times that of Condition C. At 3-step latency (the maximum training delay), the Baseline retains only approximately 21% of its zero-latency return, compared to 43% for Condition B and 58% for Condition C. At 5-step latency (out-of-distribution), the Baseline's performance has effectively collapsed, with mean forward velocity approaching zero. This degradation pattern is consistent with the theoretical analysis of partially observable MDPs under observation delay: without any mechanism to account for the delay, the policy's action selection is based on a state that is increasingly stale, leading to systematic errors that compound over time and ultimately destabilize the locomotion gait.

## Computational Cost and Implementation Complexity

The three conditions differ substantially in their implementation complexity and computational requirements, with important practical implications for real robotic deployment. **Condition A (Baseline MLP)** represents the simplest architecture: a standard two-hidden-layer MLP with 256 units per layer for both actor and critic, operating on the raw 17-dimensional HalfCheetah state vector. This architecture requires no modifications to the standard SAC algorithm, uses a conventional replay buffer storing independent (s, a, r, s') transitions, and imposes minimal computational overhead. **Condition B (History-MLP)** requires only a minor modification: the observation vector is augmented with the last *k* = 3 actions (adding 18 dimensions for a 6-dimensional action space), yielding a 35-dimensional input. The MLP architecture, replay buffer, and training procedure remain otherwise identical to Condition A. This augmentation adds negligible computational cost — the larger input layer increases parameter count by a modest amount — while requiring only a simple action history buffer in the environment wrapper. The implementation overhead is minimal, making Condition B an attractive practical choice for real robotic systems where actuator latency is known and approximately fixed.

**Condition C (GRU-SAC)** introduces substantially greater complexity. The GRU encoder processes sequences of observations, requiring a sequence-based replay buffer that stores full episode trajectories and samples fixed-length subsequences of length *L* = 20 at training time. This buffer is more memory-intensive than the standard replay buffer and requires careful management of episode boundaries. The GRU's recurrent computation introduces sequential dependencies that prevent the parallelization of forward passes across batch elements in the same way as MLP architectures, increasing per-update wall-clock time. Gradient clipping (set to 0.5 for the GRU, compared to 1.0 for MLP conditions) is essential to prevent exploding gradients during backpropagation through time. At inference time, the GRU requires maintenance of a hidden state across timesteps, which must be reset at episode boundaries — a requirement that adds complexity to deployment pipelines. For real robotic systems, this means that the GRU policy cannot be trivially applied as a stateless function of the current observation, requiring instead a stateful inference loop. Despite these costs, the GRU's superior robustness to out-of-distribution latencies may justify this complexity in applications where actuator latency is variable or unpredictable.

## Limitations and Reliability of Conclusions

A critical limitation of this study is that all three conditions were evaluated with a **single random seed** (seed = 42). Deep reinforcement learning algorithms, and recurrent architectures in particular, are known to exhibit high variance across random seeds due to sensitivity to initial network weights, initial replay buffer contents, and stochastic environment interactions (Henderson et al., 2018). This limitation is most consequential for Condition C (GRU-SAC), whose recurrent architecture is particularly sensitive to initialization: different random seeds can lead to qualitatively different learning trajectories, with some seeds converging to high-performing policies and others becoming trapped in local optima or exhibiting training instability. The observed 15.7% advantage of Condition C over Condition B during training, and the more pronounced advantage in out-of-distribution robustness evaluation, should therefore be interpreted as indicative rather than definitive. It is plausible that with a different random seed, the ordering of Conditions B and C could be reversed, or the magnitude of the GRU's advantage could be substantially different. The ordering of Condition A below both augmented conditions is more robust to this concern, as the theoretical argument for the benefit of action-history information under actuator latency is strong and the observed performance gap is large (28.5% and 48.7% for Conditions B and C respectively). Future work should replicate these experiments with a minimum of five random seeds per condition to establish statistically reliable conclusions, particularly regarding the relative merits of history stacking versus learned temporal representations.
